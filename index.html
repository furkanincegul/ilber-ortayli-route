Ilber Ortaylı Route

Ilber Ortaylı

About

Ilber Ortaylı was a distinguished historian specialising in Ottoman history, modernisation, administrative history and urban history, respected both in Turkey and internationally. Born in Bregenz, Austria, in 1947, he shaped his education at Ankara University, Vienna and Chicago. He taught at Ankara University, METU, Bilkent and Galatasaray University, and stood out for his work on the late Ottoman period, the Tanzimat and state structure. Having served as director of the Topkapı Palace Museum, Ortaylı disseminated his historical knowledge beyond academia. His original approach to Ottoman modernisation attracted international interest; his works were read in different languages and contributed to making Turkish historiography visible worldwide.

Why Is This Route Special?

Because this route reads Istanbul not as a mere list of tourist stops, but as a living historical text. The city's true magic lies not only in its monuments but also in its side streets, its slopes, its neighbourhood fabric and the memory accumulated over centuries. Along this line from Eminönü to Chora, the visitor walks inside the grand urban story where trade, architecture, faith, neighbourhood life and imperial heritage are all interwoven. This is what makes Ilber Ortaylı's reading of the city special: presenting Istanbul not as a view to be glanced at, but as a civilisation text that deepens step by step.

Eminönü Square
Starting point

Used as the Neorion harbour during the Byzantine era, this area became Istanbul's busiest trade centre after the Ottoman conquest. For centuries it has served as the commercial heart of the city on the Golden Horn shore.

Spice Bazaar
Spice and trade

Built in 1660 by Hatice Turhan Sultan as part of the New Mosque complex, named after the Egyptian tax revenues that financed it. For four centuries it has symbolised the spice, herbal remedy and Turkish delight trade.

Tahtakale Market Fabric
Ottoman bazaar tradition

Centre of artisan guilds since Fatih Sultan Mehmet's reign, Tahtakale houses one of the most authentic examples of Ottoman bazaar culture. Its narrow streets and hans keep a five-century-old trade tradition alive.

Rüstem Pasha Mosque
İznik tile art

Built by Mimar Sinan in 1563 for Kanuni's grand vizier Rüstem Pasha, the mosque holds the richest collection of İznik tile art. Thousands of tiles on its walls represent the pinnacle of 16th-century Ottoman craftsmanship.

Büyük Valide Han Area
Han architecture

Commissioned by Kösem Sultan in 1651, it is Istanbul's largest han. The three-courtyard structure reflects the grand architecture of Ottoman trade hans and still houses various artisans today.

6
Süleymaniye Mosque & Complex
Mimar Sinan's masterpiece

Completed by Mimar Sinan in 1557, the Süleymaniye is the pinnacle of Ottoman architecture. The complex — mosque, madrasas, bathhouse, hospital, soup kitchen and library — was designed as a city within a city.

7
Süleymaniye Side Streets
Neighbourhood texture

The neighbourhood around the complex has lived to the rhythm of daily life for centuries. Wooden houses, fountains and small mosques carry the traces of Ottoman neighbourhood culture into the present.

Küçükpazar Passage
Historic transition zone

This passage opens from the Golden Horn shore to the inner districts of the historic peninsula, at the crossroads of trade routes from Byzantium to the Ottomans. Traces of Fatih-era walls can still be felt here.

9
Zeyrek Mosque (Pantokrator)
Byzantine heritage

Built in the 12th century by Byzantine Empress Eirene as a monastery complex, converted into a mosque after the conquest. This complex of three churches is the largest Byzantine religious structure in Istanbul after Hagia Sophia.

10
Valens Aqueduct Vista
Roman aqueduct

Built in AD 368 by Roman Emperor Valens, it is one of the city's oldest surviving monuments. Stretching approximately 900 metres, the aqueduct symbolises an unbroken urban memory from Rome through Byzantium to the Ottomans.

11
Fatih Mosque
Fatih Sultan Mehmet's mosque

The first major Ottoman complex, built in 1470 by Fatih Sultan Mehmet after the conquest. Destroyed in the 1766 earthquake and rebuilt under Mustafa III. It represents the spirit of the conquest and the Ottomans' first grand architectural statement.

12
Çarşamba / Yavuz Sultan Selim
Traditional neighbourhood

Yavuz Sultan Selim Mosque was built in 1522 on one of the city's highest hills and is famous for its Golden Horn views. The Çarşamba district offers one of the most vivid examples of traditional Istanbul neighbourhood life.

13
Balat Street Fabric
Cosmopolitan memory

One of Istanbul's most cosmopolitan districts, where Jewish, Greek and Armenian communities lived side by side. Its colourful wooden houses, synagogues and churches carry the memory of a multicultural past.

14
Fener Greek Patriarchate
Ecumenical Patriarchate

The centre of world Orthodox Christianity, the Fener Greek Patriarchate has been at this location since 1601. It has served as the administrative and religious centre of the Greek community within the Ottoman millet system for centuries.

15
Sveti Stefan Church
The Iron Church

Prefabricated in Vienna in 1898 and shipped to Istanbul via the Danube, the Bulgarian Iron Church is one of the few entirely iron-built churches in the world — a symbol of the Bulgarian community's church independence struggle.

16
Ayvansaray City Walls
Land walls and shoreline

Built in the 5th century under Byzantine Emperor Theodosius II, the land walls protected Istanbul for a thousand years. The Ayvansaray district sits at the strategic point where the walls meet the Golden Horn shore.

17
Edirnekapı
Historic wall gate

One of the most important gates of the land walls, Edirnekapı is where the Ottoman army entered the city in 1453. It has gone down in history as the symbolic gate of the conquest. The Mihrimah Sultan Mosque stands right beside it.

Open in Maps
18
Chora Mosque
Byzantine mosaics

Restored in the 14th century by Theodore Metochites, the Chora Monastery houses the most magnificent examples of Byzantine mosaic and fresco art. The depictions of Christ and the Virgin on its walls are considered among the most important works of late Byzantine art worldwide.
